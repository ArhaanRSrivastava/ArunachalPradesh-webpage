Physical Features

Geography of Arunachal Pradesh



Arunachal Pradesh, also known as the "Land of Dawn-Lit Mountains," lies in northeastern India. Characterized by its mountainous terrain, lush green valleys, and numerous rivers, the state borders Bhutan to the west, China to the north, and Myanmar to the east. Itanagar serves as the capital city. Nestled in the Himalayan foothills, Arunachal Pradesh boasts a varied landscape. Most of the state is mountainous, with some plains in the south. Elevations range from a few hundred meters to over 7,000 meters. Major rivers like the Kameng, Subansiri, and Siang flow through the state, eventually draining into the Brahmaputra River. Arunachal Pradesh is a biodiversity hotspot, rich with a variety of forests, including tropical, subtropical, temperate, and alpine ecosystems. Over 4,000 species of flowering plants, 600 species of birds, and numerous mammals, reptiles, and insects call this state home. Around 80% of the state is covered by forests, with protected areas like national parks and wildlife sanctuaries established to conserve this rich tapestry of life. The climate varies depending on elevation, with lower areas experiencing hot and humid summers and mild winters, while higher elevations are colder with subarctic climates. The state receives heavy rainfall during the monsoon season, from May to September.





Topographic Features


Arunachal Pradesh, a state crisscrossed by the mighty Himalayas, boasts stunning river valleys carved by rushing rivers. Each valley, from the snow-capped mountains of Tawang to the tropical rainforests in the lower elevations, offers distinct geographical features and climates. Lush green hills embrace these valleys, cradling unique ecosystems, cultures, and diverse wildlife. The five main valleys – Kameng, Subansiri, Siang, Lohit, and Tirap – are the lifeblood of the state, renowned for their scenic beauty, from the high-altitude pastures of the Siang Valley to the mist-shrouded forests of the Subansiri Valley. This rich biodiversity and natural beauty make Arunachal Pradesh a paradise for nature enthusiasts.


Tourism

Tawang Monastery


The Tawang Monastery is one of the largest monasteries in India and a major center of Mahayana Buddhism. Founded by Merak Lama Lodre Gyatso in 1680, it belongs to the Gelug school of Buddhism. Located in the Tawang district, it sits atop a hill offering breathtaking views of the valley below. The three-story monastery houses 450 monks and features a library with valuable scriptures. The main temple, Dukhang, has an 18-foot Buddha statue and a thangka painted with the 5th Dalai Lama's blood. The monastery is threatened by landslides but remains a significant cultural center.
















Ziro Valley


Ziro, nestled in Arunachal Pradesh's Lower Subansiri district and named after its bordering village, boasts breathtaking scenery with lush hills, dense forests, and the meandering Subansiri River. This high-altitude haven offers pleasant year-round weather, diverse wildlife, and crystal-clear waters ideal for adventure enthusiasts. Ziro Valley is known for its picturesque landscapes, lush rice fields, and the unique Apatani tribal culture. It is a perfect destination for those seeking tranquility and an immersive cultural experience. The Ziro Music Festival, held annually, attracts music lovers from all over the country.


Wildlife

Namdapha National Park


Namdapha National Park, located in the Changlang district, is a biodiversity hotspot. It is home to many endangered species such as the Snow Leopard, Clouded Leopard, and Red Panda. The park's dense forests, numerous rivers, and varied altitudes create a habitat that supports a wide range of flora and fauna. Namdapha National Park, a wilderness bordering Myanmar, covers 1,985 sq km. Lush with diverse plants and animals, it lies along the Noa-Dihing River near Miao. Declared a Tiger Reserve in 1983, it's a haven for wildlife. Namdapha status as the sole sanctuary on the planet where four distinct species of big cats, namely big cat namely the Tiger (Panthera Tigris), Leopard (Panthera Pardus), Snow Leopard (Panthera Uncia) and Clouded Leopard (Neofelis Nebulosa) roam freely.











Sela Pass


Sela Pass, at 4,170 meters, is a high mountain pass in Arunachal Pradesh, India. It connects Tawang to Dirang and Guwahati. The pass experiences limited vegetation and frequent snow cover. Sela Lake, near the summit, is one of many sacred lakes in the area, which remains frozen during the winter months. The pass is known for its role in the Sino-Indian War and the bravery of Jaswant Singh Rawat. It is also known for its breathtaking views and serene environment. The pass is a popular destination for tourists and adventure enthusiasts.
